Integrative Epidemiology Of Obesity Group

A) We study the effects of increased adiposity on human health

Increased adiposity has clear observational associations with health including all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, type-2-diabetes and cancer. Major gaps exist in understanding the mechanisms that may underlie these relationships. We use cross-disciplinary approaches - from wet lab, dry lab, cohort studies, and a variety of -omics techniques - to explore these relationships and to identify potential therapeutic targets. This research aims to flag causal targets for further investigation and identify potential pathological processes.

-- Our primary research questions are:

  1. How does obesity influence health outcomes?
  2. How does type 2 diabetes influence cancer outcomes?
  3. Does the gut microbiome causes disease?

B) We also study the interaction between environment, lifestyle, and health.

Air and noise pollution are just a few environmental exposures, alongside lifestyle, diet, education and many more that may influence health and disease. As members of the LongITOOLS study we are studying the exposome, or all of the environmental exposure an individual experiences through life, to quantify its impact on health. See more information here.

-- Our primary research questions are:

  1. How the exposome influences growth trajectories?
  2. How the exposome influences adiposity trajectories?
  3. How the exposome influences metabolic and cardiovascular health trajectories?

We are based at the University of Bristol's School of Cellular and Molecular Medicine and the Bristol Medical School. We also oversee and work closely with the Avon Longitudunal Study of Parents and Children (ALSPAC) cohort while also maintaing a close association with the Medical Research Council Integrative Epidemiology Unit (MRC-IEU).

Dr. Emma Vincent co-leads the The Elizabeth Blackwell Institute for Health Research "Integrating Health Research: Mechanisms to Populations" strand. The strand provides a formal structure for promoting and facilitating interdisciplinary research between fundamental bioscience and population health science.
